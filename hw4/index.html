Overview

In this homework I built a real-time cloth simulator from scratch using a mass-spring system. I started by discretizing the cloth into a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs. At each timestep I compute the forces on every point mass from gravity and the spring network, then use Verlet integration to step the positions forward in time. I also added a Provot-style constraint that prevents any spring from stretching more than 10% past its rest length in a single step, which keeps things stable.

On top of the core simulation I implemented collision handling with spheres and planes, and self-collision detection using spatial hashing so the cloth doesn't clip through itself. Finally I wrote a set of GLSL shaders covering diffuse lighting, Blinn-Phong shading, texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, and environment-mapped reflections.

One of the coolest things I got out of this project was seeing how much visual realism you can squeeze out of a pretty simple physical model. Just changing the spring constant or density produces wildly different cloth behavior, and the shaders add a ton of perceived detail at almost no simulation cost.

Part 1: Masses and springs

To build the cloth grid I iterate over a num_height_points x num_width_points array of point masses in row-major order. For a horizontal cloth I set y = 1 and vary positions over the xz plane. For a vertical cloth I vary positions over the xy plane and add a small random z offset between -1/1000 and 1/1000 to break symmetry. Any mass whose (x, y) grid index shows up in the pinned list gets marked as pinned and stays fixed during simulation.

Once all the masses are placed I add the springs. Structural springs connect each mass to the one to its left and the one directly above it. Shearing springs connect diagonally to the upper-left and upper-right neighbors. Bending springs connect two positions to the left and two positions above, and I scale their spring constant down to 20% of ks to make them weaker than the other two types.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

At each timestep I first accumulate forces on every point mass. I convert external accelerations like gravity into forces using F = ma and apply them to every point. Then I go through all the springs and apply Hooke's law: \[ F_s = k_s \cdot (||p_a - p_b|| - l) \cdot \hat{d} \] where \( l \) is the rest length and \( \hat{d} \) is the unit vector from \( p_a \) to \( p_b \). Bending springs get multiplied by 0.2 to keep them weaker.

For position updates I use Verlet integration with a damping term: \[ x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1 - d)(x_t - x_{t-dt}) + a_t \cdot dt^2 \] where \( d \) is the damping percentage divided by 100. After the integration step I apply the Provot constraint: if a spring's length is more than 1.1x its rest length, I correct the two endpoint positions to bring it back in range. I split the correction evenly between both masses, unless one end is pinned, in which case I apply all of it to the free mass.

Effect of spring constant (ks)

When I set ks to something low like 500 N/m, the cloth gets really loose and stretchy. It sags a lot in the middle and has a bunch of ripples as it settles. At a high ks like 50,000 N/m the cloth barely sags at all and ends up almost flat between the two pinned corners. The default 5000 N/m is a good middle ground that gives a natural looking drape.

Effect of density

Cranking up the density makes the cloth heavier relative to the springs, so it sags more and the folds get bigger. Lowering density does the opposite and the cloth ends up much flatter since gravity has less of an effect on it.

Effect of damping

With very low damping the cloth swings and oscillates for a long time before settling, kind of like a pendulum. With high damping it just slowly drifts down to rest with no bouncing at all. The default 0.2% hits a nice balance where it settles naturally in a few seconds.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

For sphere collisions, each timestep I check if a point mass's current position is inside the sphere by seeing if its distance to the origin is less than the radius. If it is, I compute the tangent point (the spot on the sphere surface along the ray from the origin through the point mass), then compute a correction vector from the point mass's last position to that tangent point and scale it down by friction: \[ x_{new} = x_{last} + (1 - f) \cdot (tangent - x_{last}) \] This pushes the mass back to just outside the sphere surface, with friction slowing it down a bit.

For plane collisions I check if the point mass crossed the plane between the last and current timestep by seeing if the signed distances from the plane changed sign. If it crossed I find the exact intersection point along the straight-line path, offset it slightly above the surface by SURFACE_OFFSET to keep it from sinking in, and apply the same friction-scaled correction.

Effect of ks on sphere collision

At ks = 500 N/m the cloth is very floppy and wraps tightly around the sphere, hanging low with a lot of folds at the bottom. At the default ks = 5000 N/m it drapes nicely over the sphere with smooth folds. At ks = 50,000 N/m the cloth is stiff enough that it barely follows the sphere's curvature and ends up looking more like a tent sitting on top of it.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

The naive way to do self-collision is to check every pair of point masses, but that's O(n^2) and way too slow for real-time. Instead I used spatial hashing. At each timestep I build a hash map that groups point masses into 3D box buckets. Each position gets mapped to a box of size \( w \times h \times t \) where \( w = 3 \cdot width / num\_width\_points \), \( h = 3 \cdot height / num\_height\_points \), and \( t = \max(w, h) \). I compute a unique float key from the box coordinates using large prime coefficients to avoid too many collisions in the hash map.

For each point mass I look up the others in the same bucket. If any candidate is within \( 2 \times thickness \) distance I compute a correction vector to push them apart. I average all the pairwise corrections and scale by 1/simulation_steps to keep things smooth and stable.

Effect of density and ks on self-collision

With higher density the cloth is heavier so it falls faster and piles up more compactly at the bottom. Lower density makes it drift down slowly and spread out more as it folds. With a low ks the cloth crumples into a lot of small tight folds when it hits itself. A high ks makes it stiffer so it forms fewer, larger folds and tends to curl in smooth curves instead of crumpling.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is basically a small program that runs on the GPU in parallel for every vertex or every pixel of a mesh. The vertex shader runs once per vertex and handles things like transforming positions and normals. After rasterization, the fragment shader runs once per pixel and uses the interpolated per-vertex data to output a final color. Together they let you get real-time lighting and material effects that would be way too slow to do on the CPU the way we did in Homework 3.

Blinn-Phong shading

Blinn-Phong breaks lighting into three components: \[ L = k_a I_a + k_d \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, n \cdot l) + k_s \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, n \cdot h)^p \] The ambient term is just a flat constant to make sure nothing is completely black. The diffuse term depends on the angle between the surface normal and the light direction, giving that matte look. The specular term uses the halfway vector \( h = \text{normalize}(l + v) \) between the light and view directions, and the exponent p controls how tight the highlight is. Higher p gives a sharper, shinier-looking reflection.

Texture mapping

The texture shader just samples a 2D texture at the UV coordinates that get interpolated from the vertex shader and outputs that as the fragment color. Pretty straightforward to implement once you have the UVs set up.

Bump and displacement mapping

Both of these use a height map texture to fake surface detail. For bump mapping I don't change the geometry at all. Instead I perturb the surface normal in the fragment shader based on the local gradient of the height map. I build a TBN matrix from the interpolated tangent and normal vectors, then compute the height gradient: \[ dU = (h(u + 1/w, v) - h(u, v)) \cdot k_h \cdot k_n \] \[ dV = (h(u, v + 1/h) - h(u, v)) \cdot k_h \cdot k_n \] From that I get a perturbed local normal \( n_o = (-dU, -dV, 1) \) and transform it back to world space using the TBN matrix. Using that in the Phong lighting calculation is what gives the illusion of bumps.

Displacement mapping actually moves the vertex positions in the vertex shader along the surface normal, scaled by the height map value and kh. This creates real geometry changes you can see in silhouettes, not just in the shading. I then use the same bump mapping normal calculation in the fragment shader to keep the lighting consistent.

The biggest difference shows up at the silhouette and at low mesh resolution. At -o 16 -a 16 the bump map looks decent but the sphere outline is still perfectly round, while displacement mapping makes the outline clearly bumpy. At -o 128 -a 128 the displacement is much smoother and more detailed compared to the blocky low-res version.

Mirror / environment-mapped reflections

For the mirror shader I compute the outgoing eye ray from the camera to the fragment, reflect it across the surface normal using GLSL's built-in reflect() function, and sample a cubemap in the reflected direction. This gives you a pretty convincing mirror effect by assuming all the incoming light comes from an infinitely distant environment.
